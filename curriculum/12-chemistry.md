# Chemistry — Beginner Curriculum Outline

A first-ever-chemistry course for complete beginners (see `docs/content-standard.md`). No calculus,
no heavy math; ideas and pictures first, everything defined from scratch. Each module below becomes
a sub-topic; each generates 3-4 lessons with readings + diagrams + questions, tiered easy→hard.
All questions are multiple-choice. Keep arithmetic clean and correct; teach the particle/atom picture
visually.

### Module 1: What Chemistry Is — Matter and the Particle View
- Concepts: chemistry as the study of matter — what stuff is made of and how it changes; matter =
  anything with mass and volume; the big idea that all matter is made of tiny particles (atoms) in
  constant motion; pure substances vs mixtures; physical properties vs chemical properties; physical
  change (melting, dissolving — same substance) vs chemical change (burning, rusting — new substance);
  signs of a chemical change (color, gas, heat, precipitate); chemistry all around us (cooking, cleaning,
  bodies, batteries). Safety/observation mindset.

### Module 2: Atoms — The Building Blocks
- Concepts: the atom as the smallest unit of an element; a short, friendly history (from "indivisible"
  idea to the modern picture); the three parts — protons (+), neutrons (0) in a tiny dense nucleus, and
  electrons (−) in the space around it; atomic number = number of protons = which element it is; mass
  number = protons + neutrons; isotopes (same element, different neutron count) in plain terms; ions
  (atoms that gained/lost electrons → charge); the atom is mostly empty space; relative sizes/scale.

### Module 3: Elements and the Periodic Table
- Concepts: an element = one kind of atom; element symbols and names; the periodic table as the
  organized "map" of all elements; periods (rows) and groups/families (columns) and why elements in a
  group behave alike; metals vs nonmetals vs metalloids; famous groups in plain terms (alkali metals
  reactive, noble gases unreactive, halogens); reading a table cell (symbol, atomic number, atomic
  mass); periodic trends as gentle ideas (size, reactivity) without formulas; the table tells a story.

### Module 4: Inside the Atom — Electrons and Why Atoms React
- Concepts: electrons arranged in shells/energy levels (inner fills first); the outer shell (valence)
  electrons are what matter for chemistry; the "octet"/full-shell idea — atoms are most stable with a
  full outer shell, and that drive to fill it is why atoms react; why noble gases are unreactive (already
  full); valence electrons predict how many bonds an atom forms; a gentle picture of electron "clouds"
  (no orbital math). Sets up bonding.

### Module 5: Chemical Bonds — How Atoms Stick Together
- Concepts: a bond = atoms held together by sharing or transferring electrons to reach stable full
  shells; IONIC bonding (a metal gives electrons to a nonmetal → +/− ions attract, e.g. table salt);
  COVALENT bonding (nonmetals share electrons, e.g. water, oxygen); METALLIC bonding (a "sea" of shared
  electrons → why metals conduct and bend) in plain terms; polar vs nonpolar covalent as "shared
  unequally vs equally" (why water is special); intermolecular forces lightly (why things stick, boil).

### Module 6: Molecules, Compounds, Formulas and Names
- Concepts: molecules and compounds (atoms bonded together); chemical formulas (H₂O, CO₂, NaCl) — what
  the symbols and subscripts mean (how many of each atom); element vs compound vs mixture recap;
  diatomic molecules (O₂, N₂); simple naming basics in plain terms (ionic name patterns, common molecule
  names) without exhaustive rules; reading a formula to count atoms; models of molecules (ball-and-stick).

### Module 7: Counting Atoms — The Mole and Amounts
- Concepts: the problem — atoms are unimaginably tiny and numerous, so we count them in huge groups; the
  MOLE as "a chemist's dozen" (a fixed huge number, Avogadro's number, ~6×10²³) — a convenient counting
  unit; molar mass = the mass of one mole, read off the periodic table; converting between grams, moles,
  and number of particles as plain proportional reasoning with clean examples; why the mole lets us
  weigh out the right amounts for a reaction. Keep arithmetic simple and correct.

### Module 8: Chemical Reactions and Equations
- Concepts: a reaction rearranges atoms into new substances; reactants → products; conservation of mass
  (atoms aren't created or destroyed — they're rearranged); reading and BALANCING a simple equation (same
  count of each atom on both sides) with easy worked examples; common reaction types in plain terms
  (combination, decomposition, combustion, single/double replacement); recognizing a reaction happened
  (recap signs); coefficients vs subscripts. Light, intuitive, clean numbers.

### Module 9: States of Matter and Phase Changes
- Concepts: solid, liquid, gas (and a mention of plasma) explained by particle spacing/motion/energy;
  phase changes (melting, freezing, boiling/evaporation, condensation, sublimation) as adding/removing
  energy; melting and boiling points; what temperature really is (average particle motion); heating
  curves in plain terms (energy goes into breaking bonds during a phase change, not raising temperature);
  why water expands when it freezes (briefly). Everyday hooks (ice, steam, dry ice).

### Module 10: Gases and How They Behave
- Concepts: gases as fast, far-apart particles bouncing around; pressure = particles hitting walls;
  the intuitive gas relationships in plain words — squeeze a gas (less volume → more pressure), heat a
  gas (more pressure or volume), more gas → more pressure; temperature/volume/pressure trade-offs
  (Boyle/Charles ideas conceptually, no formulas required, maybe one clean proportion); everyday hooks
  (balloons, tires, spray cans, breathing); why warnings about heating sealed containers.

### Module 11: Mixtures, Solutions and Concentration
- Concepts: mixtures (uniform/homogeneous vs non-uniform/heterogeneous); solutions = a solute dissolved
  in a solvent (salt in water); why things dissolve ("like dissolves like" — polar/nonpolar, plain);
  solubility and saturation (how much dissolves; temperature effects); concentration in plain terms
  (dilute vs concentrated; the idea of amount-per-volume) with clean simple examples; separating mixtures
  (filtering, evaporation, distillation) in everyday terms; suspensions/colloids lightly (milk, fog).

### Module 12: Acids, Bases and the pH Scale
- Concepts: acids and bases in plain terms (sour vs slippery/bitter; common examples — lemon/vinegar vs
  soap/baking soda); what they do chemically (acids release H⁺-type behavior; bases the opposite) kept
  intuitive; the pH scale 0–14 (7 = neutral, lower = more acidic, higher = more basic) and that each step
  is a big jump; indicators (litmus, cabbage juice — color shows pH); neutralization (acid + base → salt
  + water, fizzing antacids); acids/bases in daily life and safety. No equilibrium math.

### Module 13: Energy, Rates and Equilibrium in Reactions
- Concepts: reactions absorb or release energy — EXOTHERMIC (releases heat: burning, hand warmers) vs
  ENDOTHERMIC (absorbs heat: cold packs, cooking); activation energy as the "hill" a reaction must climb
  (a spark to start a fire); what speeds reactions up (REACTION RATE) — temperature, concentration,
  surface area, and CATALYSTS (helpers that speed things without being used up, e.g. enzymes) in plain
  terms; reversible reactions and a gentle idea of equilibrium (forward and back balance out). All
  conceptual, no math.

### Module 14: Carbon and the Chemistry of Life (Organic Intro)
- Concepts: why carbon is special (forms four bonds, chains and rings → endless molecules); organic
  chemistry = the chemistry of carbon compounds; hydrocarbons (fuels — methane, propane, gasoline) in
  plain terms; the idea of functional groups as "the active part" lightly; the big biological molecules
  for beginners — carbohydrates, fats, proteins, DNA — as carbon-based molecules that run living things;
  polymers (plastics, and natural ones) as long repeating chains; everyday hooks (food, fuel, plastic).

### Module 15: Chemistry Everywhere — Materials, Environment, and a Peek Beyond
- Concepts: chemistry in daily life and industry — medicines, cleaning, food, batteries, materials
  (metals, ceramics, plastics, semiconductors lightly); environmental chemistry in plain terms (air/water,
  greenhouse gases, the ozone idea, recycling and green chemistry); a gentle peek at nuclear chemistry
  (radioactivity, the nucleus changing, uses and cautions) — distinct from chemical reactions; how to
  keep learning chemistry. Inspirational, conceptual wrap-up tying the course together.
