# Networking — Lesson Plan

This module sits at the hinge of your curriculum. It pulls forward the digital and analog signaling intuition from topics 02–03 (lines, levels, eye diagrams, encoding), leans on the Linux foundation from topic 05 (where most of these protocols live as `ip`, `ss`, `tcpdump`, `iptables`), and feeds two directions at once: down into the embedded world of topic 04 (network stacks on MCUs, IoT) and forward into RF and wave propagation in topic 07 (the wireless module here is deliberately the on-ramp — WiFi/BLE/LoRa/cellular PHY, modulation, and antennas hand off directly to propagation theory). Your radar background is a genuine asset: framing, timing, modulation, link budgets, and noise are not new ideas to you — networking just dresses them in new vocabulary and protocol layers.

Prerequisites: comfort with binary/hex and bit manipulation (topic 01/03), basic Linux shell and processes (topic 05), and enough programming to write a socket client/server (you have this). The plan is REFRESH + MODERNIZE: we re-derive fundamentals quickly because you asked for everything, then spend real time on the modern stack (QUIC, IoT protocols, SDN, observability, TLS 1.3) that has shifted since most EE programs last touched networking. Work the modules in order — each assumes the framing of the previous one.

## Learning outcomes

By the end of this module you will be able to:

- Map any real protocol or problem to the correct layer of both the OSI and TCP/IP models, and explain why the models disagree.
- Reason from physical signaling (encoding, clock recovery, impedance) up through framing, addressing, routing, and application semantics as one continuous chain.
- Subnet IPv4 and IPv6 in your head for common cases and on paper for arbitrary ones; design an addressing plan for a small organization.
- Choose between TCP, UDP, and QUIC for a given workload and justify it on latency/reliability/ordering grounds.
- Write socket code (TCP, UDP, TLS) and read your own traffic in Wireshark/tcpdump, identifying handshakes, retransmissions, and failures.
- Diagnose a broken network methodically (link → IP → DNS → transport → application) using ping, traceroute, dig, ss, and packet capture.
- Explain DNS, DHCP, NAT, and TLS 1.3 mechanically — not just what they do but the exact message exchanges.
- Describe the PHY of WiFi, BLE, LoRa, and cellular well enough to estimate range/throughput tradeoffs and connect them to propagation and link-budget thinking (topic 07).
- Bring up a network stack on a microcontroller (lwIP or equivalent) and speak MQTT/CoAP to a broker.
- Reason about latency, throughput, bufferbloat, and congestion control quantitatively.

## Module breakdown

### Module 1: Models, layering, and the shape of the problem
- **Concepts:** The fundamental problem networking solves: move bits between processes on different machines reliably-enough, addressed correctly, over imperfect shared media. The OSI 7-layer model (Physical, Data Link, Network, Transport, Session, Presentation, Application) as a *teaching* taxonomy. The TCP/IP 4-layer model (Link, Internet, Transport, Application) as the *real* one. Why they don't line up (OSI's session/presentation layers mostly evaporated into applications and libraries). Encapsulation and decapsulation: headers as nested envelopes; the same payload gaining and shedding headers as it descends and ascends the stack. Protocol data units per layer (frame, packet, segment/datagram). The end-to-end principle and why intelligence lives at the edges. Standards bodies and where specs come from: IETF/RFCs, IEEE 802, ITU.
- **Why it matters / connections:** Layering is the mental filing cabinet for everything that follows; every later module slots into a layer. The "envelopes inside envelopes" model is exactly the framing/encoding nesting you saw in radar pulse trains and digital protocols (topic 03). It also tells you *where to look* when something breaks.
- **Hands-on / exercises:** Take a single HTTP request and on paper draw every header it accumulates from application down to Ethernet frame, with byte counts. Capture one packet in Wireshark and expand every layer, matching each to a model layer. Write a one-paragraph argument for why TLS is "really" presentation-layer but lives in the application layer in practice.
- **You've got it when:** You can be handed any protocol name (ARP, BGP, QUIC, 802.11, DHCP) and immediately say which layer it operates at and what it's encapsulated in.

### Module 2: Physical layer — signaling, encoding, and cabling
- **Concepts:** Turning bits into signals on a medium. Baseband vs broadband. Line coding: NRZ, NRZI, Manchester, 4B/5B, 8B/10B, 64B/66B, PAM (PAM-4 in modern Ethernet) — and *why* coding exists (DC balance, clock recovery from the data stream, run-length limits, error detection). Clock recovery and why self-clocking codes matter. Symbol rate (baud) vs bit rate; bits per symbol. Channel capacity and the Shannon–Hartley limit (C = B·log2(1+SNR)) — connect directly to your SNR/noise intuition from radar and topic 02. Copper media: twisted pair (Cat5e/6/6a/7/8), differential signaling and common-mode rejection, characteristic impedance (100Ω), crosstalk (NEXT/FEXT), attenuation, the 100m limit and why it exists. Connectors (RJ45, pinouts, T568A/B). Fiber: single-mode vs multimode, wavelengths (850/1310/1550nm), modal dispersion, chromatic dispersion, attenuation budgets, transceivers (SFP/SFP+/QSFP). Power over Ethernet basics. Auto-negotiation and link establishment. Bit errors, BER, and where they come from.
- **Why it matters / connections:** This is your home turf re-skinned. Eye diagrams, jitter, impedance matching, differential pairs, and SNR are radar/analog concepts (topics 02, 08). The Shannon limit reappears in the wireless module and dominates topic 07. Understanding the PHY makes the difference between "the cable is bad" hand-waving and actual diagnosis.
- **Hands-on / exercises:** Compute the Shannon capacity of a channel given bandwidth and SNR; compare to a real link rate and explain the gap (coding overhead, real-world margin). Sketch the waveform for a byte under NRZ vs Manchester vs 8B/10B and explain DC balance. Inspect a real Cat6 cable's spec sheet and identify impedance, max frequency, and attenuation. If you have hardware, view a serial line on a scope and identify the encoding.
- **You've got it when:** You can explain why 10GBASE-T uses PAM-16-ish coding and DSP equalization while 100BASE-TX got away with simple MLT-3, in terms of bandwidth and SNR.

### Module 3: Data link layer — Ethernet, framing, MAC, and switching
- **Concepts:** The MAC sublayer and LLC. MAC addresses: 48-bit structure, OUI, unicast/multicast/broadcast bit, locally-administered bit. The Ethernet II frame format byte by byte: preamble, SFD, dest/src MAC, EtherType, payload (46–1500), FCS (CRC-32); minimum/maximum frame size and *why* (collision detection timing legacy); jumbo frames. CSMA/CD history and why it's now irrelevant on switched full-duplex links (but understand it — it explains the minimum frame size). Hubs vs switches vs bridges. How a switch works: MAC learning, the CAM/forwarding table, flooding unknown unicast, broadcast/collision domains. VLANs and 802.1Q tagging (the 4-byte tag, VLAN ID, priority/PCP). Spanning Tree Protocol (STP/RSTP) and why loops are catastrophic at L2 (no TTL on Ethernet). Link aggregation (LACP). Error detection with CRC vs error correction (Ethernet detects, doesn't correct). Flow control (802.3x PAUSE).
- **Why it matters / connections:** The frame is the unit that actually crosses a wire; everything above rides inside it. CRC-32 ties to your error-detection coding knowledge. Switching is where most LAN performance and security questions live. VLANs are foundational for any real network design and for the SDN module.
- **Hands-on / exercises:** In Wireshark, dissect an Ethernet frame field-by-field and verify the FCS concept. Build a two-switch network (real or in GNS3/Containerlab) and watch the MAC tables populate with `show mac address-table` or bridge fdb. Create two VLANs and prove that hosts can't talk across them without a router. Trigger and observe STP convergence by adding a loop.
- **You've got it when:** You can trace exactly what a switch does with a frame for unknown-unicast, known-unicast, broadcast, and multicast, and explain why a L2 loop without STP melts the network.

### Module 4: ARP and the L2/L3 boundary
- **Concepts:** The address resolution problem: you have an IP, you need the MAC to build the frame. ARP request (broadcast) / reply (unicast) exchange in detail. The ARP cache, aging, and `arp -a` / `ip neigh`. Gratuitous ARP and its uses (IP conflict detection, failover/VRRP). Proxy ARP. ARP spoofing/poisoning as the canonical L2 attack and why it works (no authentication). The IPv6 equivalent: Neighbor Discovery Protocol (NDP) using ICMPv6 (Neighbor Solicitation/Advertisement, Router Solicitation/Advertisement) and why it replaced ARP. RARP/BOOTP as DHCP ancestors.
- **Why it matters / connections:** ARP is the literal glue between Module 3 (MAC) and Module 5 (IP). It's a top source of "works intermittently" bugs and a classic attack surface. NDP foreshadows IPv6 autoconfiguration (Module 8).
- **Hands-on / exercises:** Clear your ARP cache, ping a LAN host, and capture the ARP request/reply with the subsequent ICMP. Capture IPv6 NDP traffic and identify NS/NA. Read about and (in a lab only) observe ARP spoofing with `arpspoof` to internalize why it's trivial.
- **You've got it when:** You can explain what happens, packet by packet, the very first time your machine talks to a never-before-seen host on the same subnet vs one across a router.

### Module 5: Network layer — IP, ICMP, and packet forwarding
- **Concepts:** The IPv4 header field by field: version, IHL, DSCP/ECN, total length, identification/flags/fragment offset (fragmentation and reassembly, and why it's avoided today), TTL, protocol, header checksum, src/dst address, options. Fragmentation, MTU, Path MTU Discovery, and the "black hole" failure mode. TTL and how it prevents L3 loops (contrast Module 3). ICMP: echo request/reply (ping), time exceeded (traceroute's engine), destination unreachable, fragmentation needed, redirect. The IP forwarding decision: longest-prefix match against the routing table. Connectionless, best-effort delivery — no guarantees, that's the transport layer's job. The relationship between forwarding (per-packet, data plane) and routing (building the table, control plane) — a distinction the SDN module builds on.
- **Why it matters / connections:** IP is the universal "thin waist" of the Internet — everything rides over IP and IP rides over everything. Understanding the header is essential for reading captures and for fragmentation/MTU bugs, which are endemic in VPNs and tunnels. The control/data plane split is the conceptual seed of SDN.
- **Hands-on / exercises:** Decode an IP header from a hex dump by hand. Use `ping -M do -s <size>` to find your path MTU experimentally. Capture and explain how traceroute uses incrementing TTL and ICMP time-exceeded. Read your host's routing table (`ip route`) and predict which interface a given destination uses.
- **You've got it when:** You can explain why a working ping but failing large transfer almost always means a Path MTU/fragmentation problem.

### Module 6: IPv4 addressing and subnetting
- **Concepts:** Dotted-decimal notation and its binary reality. Classful addressing (A/B/C) as history, and CIDR as the present. Prefix length / netmask equivalence; converting between /n, dotted-decimal mask, and wildcard mask. Network address, broadcast address, usable host range, host count = 2^(32−n) − 2. Subnetting: splitting a block into equal subnets; VLSM (variable-length subnet masking) for unequal subnets. Supernetting/route aggregation. Special ranges: private (RFC 1918: 10/8, 172.16/12, 192.168/16), loopback (127/8), link-local (169.254/16), documentation, multicast (224/4). Why we ran out of IPv4 and the role of NAT (Module 9). Reading and designing an addressing plan.
- **Why it matters / connections:** This is the single most-tested networking skill in interviews and the daily bread of network design. It's pure binary arithmetic — directly your topic 01/03 strength. NAT and the address exhaustion story motivate IPv6 (Module 8).
- **Hands-on / exercises:** Subnetting drills until automatic: given 192.168.10.0/24, carve into 4 equal subnets — list network/broadcast/range for each. Given a host count requirement per department, design a VLSM plan from a /22. Given an IP and mask, compute network and broadcast in your head. Aggregate four contiguous /24s into one prefix. Do 30+ of these; speed matters.
- **You've got it when:** Given any IP/prefix you can produce network address, broadcast, first/last usable host, and total hosts in under 20 seconds without a calculator.

### Module 7: Routing — static routes and dynamic protocols overview
- **Concepts:** The routing table revisited: destination prefix, next hop, interface, metric, administrative distance. Directly-connected vs static vs dynamic routes. Default route (0.0.0.0/0). Longest-prefix match resolution. Static routing: when and why (small/stub networks, predictability). Dynamic routing fundamentals: distance-vector vs link-state vs path-vector. RIP (distance vector, hop count, count-to-infinity, mostly historical). OSPF (link-state, Dijkstra/SPF, areas, LSAs — the dominant interior gateway protocol). IS-IS (link-state, used by large carriers). EIGRP (Cisco hybrid). BGP (path-vector, the protocol that *is* the Internet — autonomous systems, eBGP vs iBGP, path attributes, policy routing, why BGP misconfigurations cause global outages). Routing vs forwarding (control plane vs data plane) again. Convergence, route flapping, dampening. ECMP (equal-cost multipath).
- **Why it matters / connections:** Routing is how packets cross the world. You don't need to configure OSPF for most software jobs, but you must understand the concepts to reason about latency paths, multi-region systems, and outages. BGP literacy explains real-world Internet incidents.
- **Hands-on / exercises:** Configure a static route on Linux (`ip route add`) and prove it changes the path. In a GNS3/Containerlab/FRRouting lab, bring up OSPF between three routers and watch the table converge; break a link and observe reconvergence. Trace a real route to a distant server and identify AS boundaries with a looking-glass or `mtr`. Read one published BGP-leak post-mortem.
- **You've got it when:** You can explain the difference between an interior gateway protocol and BGP, and why the Internet needs policy (not just shortest-path) routing.

### Module 8: IPv6 — addressing, autoconfiguration, transition
- **Concepts:** Why IPv6: 128-bit address space, end of NAT necessity, cleaner header. Address notation: hex groups, `::` compression, rules for canonical form. Address types: global unicast, link-local (fe80::/10, always present), unique local (fc00::/7), multicast (ff00::/8, *no broadcast in IPv6*), anycast. The interface identifier and EUI-64; privacy extensions (RFC 4941) — MODERN concern. Prefix sizes: /64 as the standard subnet, /48 typical site allocation. SLAAC (stateless address autoconfiguration) via Router Advertisements vs DHCPv6 (stateful/stateless). NDP as the ARP replacement (revisit Module 4). The simplified IPv6 header and extension headers; no in-network fragmentation. ICMPv6's expanded role (it's mandatory, not optional). Dual-stack operation. Transition mechanisms: tunneling (6in4, 6to4), translation (NAT64/DNS64), happy eyeballs. Subnetting IPv6 (trivially, on nibble boundaries).
- **Why it matters / connections:** IPv6 is now majority traffic on many mobile carriers and large clouds; "IPv4-only" thinking dates you in a job market. SLAAC and NDP show a cleaner take on the problems ARP/DHCP solved. Mobile/cellular (wireless module) is heavily IPv6.
- **Hands-on / exercises:** Compress and expand several IPv6 addresses by hand. Identify your machine's link-local and global addresses (`ip -6 addr`) and explain how each was assigned. Capture Router Advertisement traffic and watch SLAAC happen. Subnet a /48 into /64s for several sites. Compare an IPv4 vs IPv6 header side by side.
- **You've got it when:** You can canonicalize any IPv6 address, explain SLAAC vs DHCPv6, and articulate why IPv6 has no broadcast and no router fragmentation.

### Module 9: NAT, DHCP, and DNS — the services that make networks usable
- **Concepts:**
  - *NAT:* SNAT/DNAT, PAT (port address translation / "NAT overload" — what your home router actually does), the connection-tracking table, port mapping, and the consequences: breaking the end-to-end principle, inbound-connection problems, hole-punching (STUN/TURN/ICE) for P2P and VoIP, Carrier-Grade NAT. Why IPv6 wants to kill NAT.
  - *DHCP:* the DORA exchange (Discover, Offer, Request, Acknowledge) in full, broadcast mechanics, lease/renewal/rebinding (T1/T2), DHCP relay (giaddr) across subnets, options (gateway, DNS, NTP, PXE/boot). DHCPv6 differences.
  - *DNS:* the hierarchy (root → TLD → authoritative), recursive vs iterative resolution, resolver/caching, record types (A, AAAA, CNAME, MX, NS, SOA, TXT, SRV, PTR/reverse), TTLs, zone files, the resolution walk for a name end to end. DNS over UDP/53 and fallback to TCP; EDNS. MODERN: DNS over HTTPS (DoH) and DNS over TLS (DoT) for privacy; DNSSEC for authenticity. Common failure modes (stale cache, split-horizon, negative caching).
- **Why it matters / connections:** "It's always DNS" is a sysadmin joke because it's true — DNS is the most common real-world failure. DHCP and DNS are what you debug first when a machine "can't get on the network." NAT explains why your home server isn't reachable and why VoIP/games need extra machinery. Ties to Linux (topic 05) `resolv.conf`, `systemd-resolved`, `dnsmasq`.
- **Hands-on / exercises:** Capture a full DHCP DORA on boot and label each packet. Use `dig +trace example.com` and narrate every step from root to answer. Set up a local `dnsmasq` providing DHCP+DNS for a lab subnet. Configure a port-forward (DNAT) on a Linux router with nftables and verify inbound reach. Resolve the same name with DoH and plain DNS and compare captures (one is opaque).
- **You've got it when:** You can debug "no internet" methodically and explain a full name resolution and a full DHCP lease without notes.

### Module 10: Transport layer — TCP, UDP, ports, and sockets
- **Concepts:** The job of transport: process-to-process delivery (ports) and optionally reliability/ordering/flow/congestion control. Port numbers (well-known/registered/ephemeral) and the 4-tuple (src IP, src port, dst IP, dst port) that identifies a connection.
  - *UDP:* the minimal 8-byte header, connectionless, unordered, unreliable, no congestion control — and why that's perfect for DNS, DHCP, VoIP, gaming, and as a substrate for QUIC.
  - *TCP:* the header field by field (seq, ack, flags SYN/ACK/FIN/RST/PSH/URG, window, checksum, options). The three-way handshake (SYN, SYN-ACK, ACK) and connection teardown (FIN/FIN-ACK, and RST). Sequence/acknowledgment numbers and reliable byte-stream semantics. Sliding window and flow control (receiver window). Congestion control: slow start, congestion avoidance, fast retransmit/fast recovery; the AIMD sawtooth; modern algorithms (CUBIC default on Linux, and MODERN: BBR which models bandwidth/RTT instead of reacting to loss). Retransmission timeout vs duplicate-ACK fast retransmit; SACK. Nagle's algorithm and delayed ACK (and their pathological interaction). TIME_WAIT and why it exists. Head-of-line blocking (motivates QUIC, Module 14).
  - *Sockets:* the Berkeley sockets API as the universal interface — socket/bind/listen/accept/connect/send/recv/close; blocking vs non-blocking; the TCP and UDP state machines as seen from the API. select/poll/epoll for scaling (topic 05 connection).
- **Why it matters / connections:** This is *the* layer software developers touch most. Sockets are your daily API. Understanding congestion control explains real throughput. TCP state (`ss -tan`) is core Linux troubleshooting (topic 05). Head-of-line blocking is the thread that leads to HTTP/2, HTTP/3, and QUIC.
- **Hands-on / exercises:** Write a TCP echo server and client in your language of choice; then a UDP one; observe the difference under packet loss (use `tc netem` to inject loss/delay — topic 05). Capture a TCP handshake and teardown in Wireshark; identify every flag. Force and observe a retransmission and a fast-retransmit. Use Wireshark's TCP stream graphs to *see* slow start and the congestion window grow. Inspect socket states with `ss` during your test.
- **You've got it when:** You can read a TCP capture and narrate the connection's life including window growth, a loss event, recovery, and teardown — and you can state when you'd reach for UDP instead.

### Module 11: TLS and security fundamentals
- **Concepts:** The CIA triad (confidentiality, integrity, availability) applied to networks. Symmetric vs asymmetric crypto, hashing, MACs/HMAC, and AEAD ciphers (AES-GCM, ChaCha20-Poly1305) — just enough to use them correctly. The TLS 1.3 handshake (MODERN — much simpler and faster than 1.2): 1-RTT handshake, ephemeral Diffie-Hellman (forward secrecy), removal of legacy ciphers, 0-RTT resumption and its replay caveat. Certificates and PKI: X.509, the chain of trust, certificate authorities, root stores, certificate validation (name match, expiry, revocation via CRL/OCSP/OCSP-stapling). SNI and Encrypted Client Hello (MODERN). What TLS does and does *not* protect (metadata, traffic analysis, DNS names pre-ECH). Common transport security: SSH (topic 05), IPsec and the VPN landscape, WireGuard (MODERN — minimal, fast). Network attack taxonomy: spoofing, MITM (revisit ARP from Module 4), replay, DoS/DDoS, amplification (DNS/NTP reflection), TCP RST injection. Defense: firewalls (stateful vs stateless), segmentation/VLANs, zero-trust networking (MODERN), TLS everywhere.
- **Why it matters / connections:** Everything is encrypted now; you cannot read modern traffic in Wireshark without understanding TLS (and you'll learn to decrypt your own with keylog files). Cert errors are a daily developer headache. WireGuard/zero-trust are where the industry is moving. Ties to Linux firewalling (nftables/iptables, topic 05).
- **Hands-on / exercises:** Capture a TLS 1.3 handshake and identify ClientHello/ServerHello, key share, and where it goes encrypted. Decrypt your own browser's TLS in Wireshark using `SSLKEYLOGFILE`. Inspect a real certificate chain with `openssl s_client -connect host:443` and walk the chain to a root. Stand up a WireGuard tunnel between two hosts and capture the (opaque) traffic. Build a stateful firewall ruleset with nftables.
- **You've got it when:** You can explain the TLS 1.3 handshake message flow, why forward secrecy matters, how certificate validation works, and what an attacker can and cannot see on an encrypted link.

### Module 12: Application protocols — HTTP and the modern web stack, plus messaging
- **Concepts:**
  - *HTTP:* the request/response model, methods, status codes, headers, statelessness and cookies/sessions. HTTP/1.0 vs 1.1 (persistent connections, pipelining and why it failed). HTTP/2 (MODERN: binary framing, multiplexed streams over one TCP connection, header compression HPACK — but still TCP head-of-line blocking). HTTP/3 (MODERN: HTTP over QUIC/UDP, eliminating TCP HOL blocking — see Module 14). REST and JSON APIs; gRPC (HTTP/2-based RPC) and Protobuf; WebSockets for bidirectional streams; Server-Sent Events. Caching, CDNs, and proxies/reverse proxies/load balancers (L4 vs L7).
  - *Messaging / IoT (MODERN, ties to embedded topic 04):* MQTT (publish/subscribe broker model, topics, QoS 0/1/2, retained messages, last-will — the dominant IoT protocol; relate to your topic 04 voice-translator pub/sub instinct). CoAP (RESTful over UDP for constrained devices, DTLS). AMQP/Kafka for backend messaging (overview). NTP/PTP for time sync (PTP is precision time — relevant to radar/instrumentation, topic 08). SNMP for device management. SMTP/IMAP overview.
- **Why it matters / connections:** As a software developer this is where you live, but the *protocol mechanics* (multiplexing, HOL blocking, pub/sub QoS) are often fuzzy. MQTT/CoAP are the bridge to embedded IoT (topic 04). The HTTP/1→2→3 evolution is the clearest case study of why the lower-layer details (TCP HOL blocking) drive application design.
- **Hands-on / exercises:** Capture an HTTP/1.1 and an HTTP/2 session and contrast framing/multiplexing in Wireshark. Hand-write an HTTP request with `nc`/`openssl s_client` to a server. Run a local MQTT broker (Mosquitto), publish/subscribe with `mosquitto_pub/sub` at different QoS levels, and capture the CONNECT/PUBLISH/SUBSCRIBE packets. Build a tiny REST service and a gRPC service and compare wire formats.
- **You've got it when:** You can explain why HTTP/2 fixed application-level HOL blocking but not transport-level, and how MQTT QoS 1 vs 2 differ on the wire.

### Module 13: Troubleshooting and observability (MODERN)
- **Concepts:** A disciplined bottom-up (or top-down) methodology: is the link up? do I have an IP/gateway? can I reach the gateway? does DNS resolve? can I open a TCP connection? does the application respond? The toolset: `ping` (reachability, RTT, loss), `traceroute`/`tracert`/`mtr` (path, per-hop latency/loss), `dig`/`nslookup` (DNS), `ip`/`ifconfig` (interfaces, addresses, routes), `ss`/`netstat` (sockets/connections), `arp`/`ip neigh`, `nc`/`ncat` (port testing), `curl -v` (HTTP), `nmap` (port/host discovery — lab/authorized only). Packet capture: `tcpdump` (BPF filter syntax, capture to pcap) and Wireshark (display filters, Follow Stream, Expert Info, IO/TCP graphs, statistics). Reading captures: identify retransmissions, resets, DNS failures, TLS alerts, MTU issues. MODERN observability: the shift from device-centric SNMP to flow-based and telemetry-based monitoring — NetFlow/IPFIX/sFlow, streaming telemetry (gNMI), Prometheus exporters for network metrics, distributed tracing (OpenTelemetry) as application-layer network observability, and eBPF-based tooling (Cilium/Hubble, `bpftrace`) for kernel-level network visibility (ties to topic 05 Linux).
- **Why it matters / connections:** Troubleshooting is the skill that makes you trusted on a team and shines in interviews. Methodology beats tool-knowledge. Observability (tracing, eBPF, flow telemetry) is a fast-growing, marketable, *modern* skill area that didn't exist when you last studied networking.
- **Hands-on / exercises:** Deliberately break things in a lab (wrong gateway, wrong DNS, blocked port, MTU mismatch) and diagnose each with the methodology, writing down the exact command that revealed the fault. Write five useful `tcpdump` BPF filters from memory. Capture a failed TLS connection and identify the alert. Set up a Prometheus node/network exporter and graph interface throughput.
- **You've got it when:** Handed a "the website is slow/down" report, you can produce a layered checklist and isolate the failure to a specific layer with the right tool on the first or second try.

### Module 14: Performance, congestion, and QUIC (MODERN)
- **Concepts:** The fundamental performance quantities: bandwidth (capacity), throughput (achieved), latency (one-way and RTT), jitter, and loss. The bandwidth-delay product and why it sets the required window size for full link utilization (TCP window scaling). Goodput vs throughput (overhead). Serialization delay, propagation delay (speed of light in fiber ≈ 2/3 c — ties to topic 07), queuing delay, processing delay. Little's Law intuition for queues. Bufferbloat (MODERN-ish but underappreciated): oversized buffers destroying latency under load; AQM solutions (CoDel, FQ-CoDel, CAKE) and ECN. Congestion control revisited: loss-based (CUBIC) vs model-based (BBR) and their fairness debates. QUIC (MODERN, major): a transport built on UDP that provides TCP-like reliability + TLS 1.3 + stream multiplexing without head-of-line blocking, with 0/1-RTT connection setup and connection migration (survives IP changes — great for mobile). Why QUIC moved transport logic into user space and what that means. Multipath (MPTCP/multipath QUIC) overview.
- **Why it matters / connections:** Performance reasoning separates engineers who *understand* networks from those who memorize commands. Bandwidth-delay product, propagation delay, and the speed of light directly connect to your radar/RF timing intuition and to topic 07. QUIC is the present and future of web transport and a strong talking point in a modern interview.
- **Hands-on / exercises:** Measure throughput with `iperf3` over a link; then add latency and loss with `tc netem` and watch throughput collapse — compute the bandwidth-delay product and explain. Demonstrate bufferbloat by saturating your uplink and pinging simultaneously, then enable FQ-CoDel and re-measure. Switch Linux congestion control from CUBIC to BBR and compare on a lossy link. Capture an HTTP/3 (QUIC) session and contrast its handshake with TCP+TLS.
- **You've got it when:** Given link bandwidth and RTT you can compute the window needed for full utilization, explain bufferbloat to a non-expert, and state concretely what QUIC improves over TCP+TLS+HTTP/2.

### Module 15: Wireless networking — the PHY on-ramp to wave propagation
*(This is the deliberate handoff to topic 07. Treat each technology as: spectrum/band, modulation, channel access, range/throughput tradeoff, antenna implications, link budget.)*
- **Concepts:**
  - *RF fundamentals shared by all:* frequency/wavelength relationship, licensed vs unlicensed bands (ISM), modulation families (ASK/FSK/PSK/QAM — connect to digital/analog topics 02–03), spread spectrum (DSSS, FHSS) and *why* it resists interference (radar relevance!), OFDM and subcarriers (the workhorse of WiFi/LTE/5G), the link budget (Tx power + antenna gains − path loss − losses = Rx power vs sensitivity), free-space path loss and the dB way of thinking, SNR/SINR and how it maps to achievable modulation/coding (MCS) and thus rate (Shannon again, Module 2), multipath/fading, MIMO and spatial multiplexing/beamforming, antenna basics (gain, directivity, polarization). This subsection is intentionally the bridge to topic 07.
  - *WiFi (802.11):* the alphabet (a/b/g/n=WiFi4/ac=WiFi5/ax=WiFi6/6E/be=WiFi7 — MODERN), 2.4/5/6 GHz bands and channels, CSMA/CA and the hidden-node problem (RTS/CTS), the 802.11 frame and association/authentication, OFDM/OFDMA, MU-MIMO, channel width (20–320 MHz), WPA2/WPA3 security.
  - *Bluetooth/BLE:* classic vs Low Energy, the 2.4 GHz GFSK PHY, adaptive frequency hopping, advertising vs connections, GATT/profiles, BLE's role in IoT and beacons, BLE mesh. Ties to embedded (topic 04).
  - *LoRa / LPWAN (MODERN, IoT):* chirp spread spectrum (CSS) and why it gives extreme range at low rate (a *radar-adjacent* modulation — chirps!), sub-GHz ISM bands, the link-budget/range/data-rate tradeoff, LoRaWAN network architecture (end device → gateway → network server), spreading factors, duty-cycle limits. NB-IoT/LTE-M as cellular LPWAN alternatives.
  - *Cellular (overview):* generations 2G→3G→4G/LTE→5G NR (MODERN), the architecture (UE, RAN/base station, core), OFDMA/SC-FDMA, frequency bands including mmWave (5G) and its propagation implications, network slicing, why cellular is heavily IPv6, and the move toward private 5G.
- **Why it matters / connections:** This is the explicit bridge to topic 07 (wave propagation) and re-engages your radar expertise — chirps (LoRa, radar pulse compression), spread spectrum, link budgets, antennas, and SNR-to-rate mapping are *exactly* your background. It's also where IoT (topic 04) physically connects to the network. Modulation and coding tie back to topics 02–03.
- **Hands-on / exercises:** Compute a link budget for a WiFi and a LoRa link and explain the orders-of-magnitude range difference from modulation/bandwidth choices. Capture WiFi management frames in monitor mode (where legal) and identify beacons/probes/associations. Scan BLE advertisements with a phone app or `bluetoothctl` and decode an advertising packet. Read a LoRa datasheet and relate spreading factor to range and airtime. (Optional, leads into topic 07/08: if you have an SDR like RTL-SDR, view a spectrum and identify WiFi/BLE/LoRa energy.)
- **You've got it when:** For any of WiFi/BLE/LoRa/cellular you can state the band, modulation, channel-access method, and the range/throughput tradeoff, and you can compute a basic link budget — and you feel the seam where this becomes topic 07.

### Module 16: Embedded networking and the modern edge (MODERN, ties to topic 04)
- **Concepts:** Networking under severe resource constraints. Lightweight TCP/IP stacks: lwIP (the canonical embedded stack — its architecture, raw/callback API vs socket API vs sequential API, memory pools, zero-copy pbufs), uIP/Contiki, FreeRTOS+TCP, the Zephyr networking stack. Hardware: Ethernet MACs and PHYs on MCUs, RMII/MII interfaces, DMA descriptors, offload engines (checksum/TCP offload). When you have *no* MAC: SLIP/PPP over serial. Constrained-device protocols (revisit Module 12): MQTT-SN, CoAP over UDP/DTLS, why pub/sub and UDP fit constrained/lossy links. 6LoWPAN (IPv6 over low-power wireless, header compression for 802.15.4) and Thread/Matter (MODERN — the converging smart-home stack). Edge/fog computing concepts. Security on constrained devices (DTLS, hardware crypto, secure boot — topic 04 connection). Power-vs-connectivity tradeoffs (duty cycling, wake-on-radio).
- **Why it matters / connections:** This is the concrete fusion of topics 04 (embedded), 05 (the host side), 06 (this), and 15's wireless PHY. Building a networked sensor end to end is one of the most demonstrable, hireable skills you can show, and it directly extends your radar-tech hardware comfort.
- **Hands-on / exercises:** Bring up lwIP (or FreeRTOS+TCP / Zephyr net) on an MCU dev board (ESP32, STM32 with Ethernet, or Pi Pico W) and ping it. Build a small networked embedded device: a sensor that publishes readings to an MQTT broker over WiFi, with TLS, and a subscriber that logs them. Capture its traffic on the host and verify the full stack from 802.11 frame up to MQTT PUBLISH. Add a last-will message and prove disconnection detection.
- **You've got it when:** You have a real device on your bench publishing telemetry through a broker, you can trace one reading from sensor read → MCU stack → wireless PHY → AP → broker → subscriber, and you can explain every layer it crossed.

### Module 17: Software-defined networking and network programmability (MODERN)
- **Concepts:** The control-plane / data-plane separation (revisit Modules 5 and 7) taken to its conclusion: centralize the control plane in a controller, leave dumb fast forwarding in switches. OpenFlow as the original SDN southbound protocol; flow tables, match/action. SDN controllers (overview). Overlay networking and tunneling: VXLAN (L2 over L3 for multi-tenant data centers), GRE, the role of encapsulation again. Network virtualization, virtual switches (Open vSwitch). Cloud/container networking (ties to topic 05/10): Linux network namespaces, veth pairs, bridges, the container networking model, Kubernetes networking and CNI (Calico/Cilium), service meshes (Envoy/Istio) as L7 SDN. eBPF/XDP for programmable, high-performance packet processing in the kernel (Cilium) — a major MODERN shift. Infrastructure as code for networks (NETCONF/YANG, gNMI, Ansible/Terraform for network config). Intent-based networking (overview).
- **Why it matters / connections:** Modern infrastructure (cloud, Kubernetes, data centers) is software-defined; for a software developer entering today's market, container/cloud networking is more immediately relevant than configuring physical routers. eBPF/XDP is one of the hottest, most marketable areas and ties straight to your Linux module (topic 05).
- **Hands-on / exercises:** Build a software network by hand with Linux network namespaces and veth pairs + a bridge, and ping across it — then realize you've built a tiny container network. Run Open vSwitch and create a flow rule. Stand up a small Kubernetes cluster (kind/k3s) and trace how a pod reaches a service (ClusterIP → kube-proxy/Cilium). Read one VXLAN packet capture and identify the inner and outer headers (encapsulation, full circle to Module 1).
- **You've got it when:** You can explain the control/data plane split as SDN realizes it, describe how two containers on different hosts communicate via an overlay, and articulate why eBPF is changing kernel networking.

## Capstone / integrative exercise

Build and fully instrument a small multi-segment network with an embedded IoT endpoint, then prove you understand every packet that crosses it. Concretely:

1. **Topology:** Using Linux (a router VM/host with two interfaces, or namespaces) create at least two subnets (e.g., a "LAN" and an "IoT" VLAN) with proper IPv4 *and* IPv6 addressing plans that you designed and documented (subnetting from Modules 6 and 8). Configure routing between them (static, plus optionally OSPF via FRRouting between two routers — Module 7).
2. **Core services:** Run DHCP + DNS for the LAN (dnsmasq), and NAT for egress (nftables) — Module 9. Stand up a local web service behind TLS 1.3 (Modules 11–12) and a Mosquitto MQTT broker.
3. **Embedded endpoint:** On a real MCU board (or an emulated one) running lwIP/Zephyr/FreeRTOS+TCP, connect over WiFi to the IoT segment, obtain an address via DHCP, resolve the broker via DNS, and publish sensor telemetry to the MQTT broker over TLS (Modules 15–16). Add a last-will message.
4. **Observe everything:** Capture traffic at multiple points. Produce an annotated trace that follows one sensor reading from the device's 802.11 frame all the way to a subscriber, naming every protocol and layer it traverses (Modules 1–16). Capture and explain: the DHCP DORA, the DNS resolution, the TLS 1.3 handshake, the MQTT CONNECT/PUBLISH, ARP/NDP, and the NAT translation for egress traffic.
5. **Performance & failure:** Use `iperf3` + `tc netem` to characterize a path (bandwidth-delay product, throughput under loss), demonstrate and then mitigate bufferbloat with FQ-CoDel (Module 14). Then deliberately inject three faults (e.g., wrong gateway, broken DNS, MTU mismatch) and document the diagnostic path for each (Module 13).
6. **Write-up:** A short report mapping the whole system to the OSI/TCP-IP layers and explaining each design choice. This is portfolio-grade and interview-ready.

A strong stretch goal: replicate the inter-host connectivity using container network namespaces / a kind cluster and a VXLAN overlay (Module 17), and compare the software-defined version to the physical one.

## Common pitfalls & rust-knockers

- **Confusing the OSI model with reality.** Memorizing "Please Do Not Throw Sausage Pizza Away" is fine, but know that TCP/IP is what runs and that TLS/session/presentation are fuzzy in practice.
- **Off-by-one subnetting errors** (forgetting the −2 for network/broadcast on IPv4, miscounting borrowed bits, mixing up mask and wildcard). Cure: volume of drills.
- **Treating IPv6 as "IPv4 with longer addresses."** No broadcast, mandatory ICMPv6, SLAAC, link-local always present, no router fragmentation — these trip up experienced people.
- **Assuming a working ping means a working application.** Ping is L3/ICMP; the app is L7 over TCP/TLS/DNS — many things between can fail.
- **Forgetting MTU/Path-MTU and fragmentation** as the cause of "small packets work, big transfers hang" — endemic with VPNs/tunnels.
- **Thinking switches route and routers switch** — keep the L2 (MAC, broadcast domain) vs L3 (IP, routing) distinction crisp.
- **Believing you can read encrypted traffic** — internalize what TLS hides and use `SSLKEYLOGFILE` to decrypt your *own* traffic for study.
- **Reaching for TCP reflexively** when UDP/QUIC fits (real-time, request/response, mobile).
- **Ignoring congestion control and the bandwidth-delay product** — then being surprised throughput is bad over a long fat link.
- **Bufferbloat blindness** — blaming "the ISP" for latency-under-load that an AQM fixes locally.
- **Stale mental model of the application layer** — not knowing HTTP/2 vs HTTP/3, gRPC, or QUIC will date you immediately in interviews.
- **Wireless hand-waving** — be able to actually compute a link budget; don't just say "more antennas = faster."
- **Tool-collecting instead of methodology** — knowing 20 tools is useless without a layered diagnostic process.

## Self-assessment checklist

- [ ] I can place any protocol at its correct layer and describe its encapsulation.
- [ ] I can explain a line code's purpose (DC balance, clock recovery) and compute Shannon capacity from bandwidth and SNR.
- [ ] I can dissect an Ethernet frame and explain switch behavior for each frame type, plus VLANs and STP's purpose.
- [ ] I can narrate the packet exchange for ARP (and NDP) on first contact with a new host.
- [ ] I can decode an IPv4 header and explain TTL, fragmentation, MTU, and Path MTU Discovery.
- [ ] I can subnet IPv4 (including VLSM) in my head for common cases and on paper for any case in seconds.
- [ ] I can canonicalize/expand IPv6 addresses and explain SLAAC vs DHCPv6 and why there's no broadcast.
- [ ] I can distinguish static/dynamic routing and explain OSPF vs BGP and the control/data plane split.
- [ ] I can explain DHCP DORA, a full DNS resolution, and NAT/PAT mechanics from memory.
- [ ] I can read a TCP capture end to end (handshake, window growth, loss/recovery, teardown) and know when to use UDP/QUIC.
- [ ] I can explain the TLS 1.3 handshake, forward secrecy, and certificate validation, and decrypt my own TLS in Wireshark.
- [ ] I can explain HTTP/1.1 vs HTTP/2 vs HTTP/3, and MQTT pub/sub with QoS levels.
- [ ] I can diagnose a broken network with a layered methodology and the right tool at each step.
- [ ] I can compute a bandwidth-delay product, explain bufferbloat, and state what QUIC improves.
- [ ] For WiFi/BLE/LoRa/cellular I can give band, modulation, channel access, range/throughput tradeoff, and a basic link budget.
- [ ] I have a real embedded device publishing telemetry over MQTT/TLS and can trace one reading across every layer.
- [ ] I can build a software network with namespaces/veth/bridges and explain SDN, overlays (VXLAN), and eBPF's role.
- [ ] I completed the capstone and produced an annotated end-to-end packet trace and write-up.
