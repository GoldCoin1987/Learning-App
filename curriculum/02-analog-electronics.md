# Analog Electronics — Lesson Plan

Analog electronics is the bedrock layer of everything physical in an electronic system: it is where the continuous, messy real world meets the discrete, idealized world of digital logic and software. For a learner with a BS in Electronics Engineering and hands-on radar technician experience, almost none of this will be conceptually new — but the working fluency (back-of-envelope biasing, instinctive impedance reasoning, knowing why a circuit oscillates when you didn't want it to) is exactly the kind of skill that goes rusty after years in software. This module is a **refresh + modernize** sweep: it re-derives the fundamentals at a brisk pace, then deliberately re-anchors them with simulation and bench work so the intuition comes back, while flagging the modern parts (low-noise integrated front-ends, precision op-amps, switch-mode power, sensor interfacing) that dominate today's analog/mixed-signal job market.

**Prerequisites (from Module 01 — Math Foundations):** complex numbers and Euler's identity (for phasors and impedance), differential and integral calculus (for transient response and reactive elements), and Fourier analysis (for frequency-domain thinking and filters). **This module feeds:** 03 Digital (logic thresholds, signal integrity), 04 Embedded (sensor interfacing, ADC front-ends, power rails), 07 Wave Propagation (transmission lines, impedance matching), and 08 Radar (low-noise receiver front-ends, mixers, detectors). Where a topic is the seed of a downstream module, it is flagged inline.

## Learning outcomes

By the end of this module you will be able to:

1. Analyze any linear DC or AC network by inspection or with systematic methods (nodal/mesh, Thevenin/Norton, superposition), and reason in both time and frequency domains fluently.
2. Predict the behavior of reactive networks (RC/RL/RLC) including transient response, resonance, Q, and bandwidth, connecting the math to physical intuition.
3. Design and analyze diode, BJT, and MOSFET circuits — biasing, small-signal models, and the standard amplifier topologies — and choose the right device/topology for a job.
4. Apply op-amps as a design primitive: gain stages, active filters, comparators, instrumentation amps, and understand the non-ideal effects that bite in practice.
5. Design passive and active filters and oscillators to specification, and reason about feedback, loop gain, phase margin, and stability.
6. Quantify and minimize noise (thermal/shot/flicker), compute noise figure, and design a low-noise signal chain — directly relevant to radar receiver front-ends.
7. Specify and regulate power: linear and switch-mode supplies, references, and rail decoupling.
8. Account for real-world parasitics, grounding, and layout, and use the core bench instruments (DMM, oscilloscope, function generator, spectrum analyzer) plus SPICE simulation competently.

## Module breakdown

### Module 1: DC Fundamentals and Network Theorems

- **Concepts:** Charge, current, voltage, power, energy; passive sign convention. Ideal voltage and current sources (independent and dependent). Resistance, conductance, resistivity; resistor color codes, tolerance, power rating, temperature coefficient. **Ohm's law** and power dissipation (P = VI = I²R = V²/R). **Kirchhoff's Current Law** (KCL) and **Voltage Law** (KVL) as conservation statements. Series and parallel resistor reduction; the voltage divider and current divider (memorize the forms cold). Wheatstone bridge and balance condition. Delta–Wye (π–T) transformations. Systematic analysis: nodal analysis and mesh analysis, and when each is less work. **Superposition** for linear circuits. **Thevenin** and **Norton** equivalents, source transformation, and **maximum power transfer** (R_load = R_th) vs. maximum voltage transfer. Internal resistance and loading effects of real meters/sources.
- **Why it matters / connections:** This is the algebra everything else is written in. Thevenin/Norton is the mental model for "what does the rest of the circuit look like from here," which recurs in amplifier input/output impedance, filter loading, and matching networks (→ 07 transmission lines). Loading effects explain why measurement (Module 16) perturbs the thing you measure.
- **Hands-on / exercises:** By hand: solve a 3-node network by nodal analysis, then verify with superposition. Reduce a ladder network to a single Thevenin equivalent. **LTspice:** build a resistive divider with a real load and confirm the loading shift vs. the unloaded prediction; sweep R_load and plot delivered power to see the max-power-transfer peak. **Bench:** measure a divider with a DMM, then with a 10× scope probe, and explain the difference from input impedance.
- **You've got it when:** You can pick nodal vs. mesh by inspection, collapse a network to Thevenin in your head for simple cases, and predict how much a 1 MΩ DMM vs. a 10 MΩ input perturbs a high-impedance node.

### Module 2: Reactive Components and Impedance

- **Concepts:** Capacitor: Q = CV, i = C dv/dt, energy ½CV², impedance Z_C = 1/(jωC), it blocks DC and passes AC, voltage cannot change instantaneously. Capacitor types and their real behavior (ceramic class I/II and DC-bias derating, film, electrolytic, tantalum, supercaps), ESR and ESL. Inductor: λ = Li, v = L di/dt, energy ½LI², impedance Z_L = jωL, it blocks AC and passes DC, current cannot change instantaneously; core materials, saturation, DCR, self-resonant frequency. Mutual inductance and transformers (turns ratio, impedance reflection, dot convention). The concept of **complex impedance** and **admittance**; reactance vs. resistance; the j-operator as a 90° rotation (ties to Euler's identity from Module 01).
- **Why it matters / connections:** Impedance is *the* unifying analog concept — once you think in Z, AC analysis becomes the same algebra as DC. ESR/ESL and self-resonance are why real decoupling works (or doesn't) — foundational for power integrity (Module 13/15) and for embedded board design (→ 04). Transformer impedance reflection is the gateway to matching networks (→ 07/08).
- **Hands-on / exercises:** Compute |Z| and phase for an R, L, C at several frequencies by hand. **LTspice:** AC-sweep a single capacitor with parasitic ESR/ESL and observe the impedance minimum at self-resonance; repeat for an inductor. **Bench:** use a function generator + scope to measure the reactance of a capacitor at two frequencies and confirm the 1/ωC trend.
- **You've got it when:** You instinctively know which way impedance moves with frequency for each element, and you can explain why a "1 µF" decoupling cap looks inductive above its self-resonant frequency.

### Module 3: AC Steady-State Analysis and Phasors

- **Concepts:** Sinusoids: amplitude, frequency, phase, angular frequency. **Phasor** representation (rotating vector ↔ complex amplitude) and why it works for linear, single-frequency steady state. Impedance and admittance in phasor form; applying KVL/KCL, nodal/mesh, Thevenin, and superposition in the phasor domain. RMS values and why we use them; average/real power P = V_rms I_rms cos φ, reactive power Q, apparent power S, the **power triangle** and power factor. Three-phase basics (conceptual). Frequency response and the transfer function H(jω); magnitude and phase. **Bode plots** (decades, dB, 20 dB/decade slopes, pole/zero asymptotes) — build this skill deliberately, it is used everywhere downstream.
- **Why it matters / connections:** Phasors collapse calculus-heavy AC problems into algebra; Bode reasoning is the language of filters, feedback stability (Module 12), and amplifier bandwidth. Power factor matters for any mains-powered design. The transfer-function/frequency-response viewpoint is the bridge to DSP (→ 03/09) and to system thinking in radar (→ 08).
- **Hands-on / exercises:** Convert time-domain expressions to phasors and back; solve an RLC network at one frequency entirely in phasor algebra. Hand-sketch the Bode plot of a one-pole and a one-zero transfer function, then **verify in LTspice** with a `.ac` analysis. **Bench:** measure gain and phase of an RC network at several frequencies and plot your own Bode magnitude curve.
- **You've got it when:** You can sketch a Bode plot from a transfer function in seconds and read poles/zeros off a measured response.

### Module 4: RC, RL, RLC Networks, Resonance and Transient Response

- **Concepts:** First-order RC and RL circuits: time constant τ = RC or L/R, natural and step response, the universal exponential, the 5τ rule. Charging/discharging, differentiator and integrator behavior of RC depending on time scale. Second-order RLC: characteristic equation, natural frequency ω₀, damping ratio ζ, **overdamped/critically damped/underdamped** responses, ringing and overshoot. **Resonance**: series resonance (impedance minimum) and parallel/tank resonance (impedance maximum), resonant frequency ω₀ = 1/√(LC). **Quality factor Q**, bandwidth, and the relationship BW = f₀/Q; selectivity. State variables (capacitor voltage, inductor current) and initial conditions; solving transients with calculus and with Laplace transforms (s-domain), tying back to Module 01.
- **Why it matters / connections:** Transient response governs signal integrity, debounce, and settling time (→ 03/04). Resonance and Q are the heart of tuned amplifiers, oscillators (Module 11), filters (Module 10), and antenna/front-end matching (→ 07/08). The Laplace/s-domain view is the formal bridge between this module and control/feedback (Module 12) and DSP (→ 03).
- **Hands-on / exercises:** Hand-derive the step response of an RC and an RLC. **LTspice:** transient-sim an RLC and vary R to walk through under/critical/overdamped; AC-sweep a series and a parallel resonant circuit, measure f₀ and Q from the −3 dB points. **Bench:** drive an RC with a square wave and observe integration/differentiation as you change frequency; build a tank circuit and find its resonance with a function generator sweep.
- **You've got it when:** You can read τ off a scope trace, identify damping regime by eye, and compute Q and bandwidth of a resonator both ways (energy ratio and −3 dB).

### Module 5: Diodes and Diode Circuits

- **Concepts:** Semiconductor basics refresh: doping, PN junction, depletion region, forward/reverse bias, the diode equation (I = I_s(e^(V/nV_T) − 1)) and the ~0.7 V (Si) / 0.3 V (Ge) / ~0.2–0.4 V (Schottky) drops. Diode models: ideal, constant-drop, piecewise-linear, small-signal (dynamic resistance r_d = nV_T/I). Reverse breakdown and **Zener** diodes (voltage reference/regulation). **Rectifiers**: half-wave, full-wave center-tapped, full-wave bridge; ripple, filter capacitor sizing, peak inverse voltage. **Voltage regulation** with a Zener (and its limits). **Clippers/limiters** and **clamps** (DC restoration). Voltage multipliers. Special diodes: Schottky (fast, low drop), LED, photodiode, varactor (voltage-variable capacitance — relevant to VCOs/tuning), PIN, tunnel/Gunn (microwave, → 08).
- **Why it matters / connections:** Rectification + filtering is the front end of every linear supply (Module 13). Clamps protect inputs (ESD, → 04). Schottky and PIN diodes appear in RF detectors and switches; varactors tune oscillators and front-ends (→ 07/08). The diode is also the conceptual stepping stone to the transistor.
- **Hands-on / exercises:** Hand-design a Zener regulator for a given load range. **LTspice:** simulate a bridge rectifier with a smoothing cap; measure ripple vs. cap value and load; build a precision clamp. **Bench:** build a full-wave rectifier, observe ripple on the scope, and verify the diode I–V curve with a curve-tracer setup or DMM diode mode.
- **You've got it when:** You can size a rectifier filter cap for a ripple spec, explain when a Zener regulator fails, and pick Schottky vs. PN for a given speed/drop tradeoff.

### Module 6: Bipolar Junction Transistors (BJT)

- **Concepts:** NPN/PNP structure and operation; the three regions (cutoff, active, saturation) and the BJT as switch vs. amplifier. β (h_FE), α, the relations I_C = βI_B, I_E = I_C + I_B; Early effect and output resistance. **DC biasing**: fixed bias, emitter-degeneration, and the workhorse **voltage-divider bias**; bias stability over β and temperature; the load line and quiescent point (Q-point). **Small-signal models**: hybrid-π and T-model, g_m = I_C/V_T, r_π, r_o. **Amplifier topologies**: common-emitter (voltage gain, inverting), common-collector/emitter-follower (buffer, high Z_in, low Z_out), common-base (wideband, low Z_in — used at RF). Gain, input/output impedance, and bandwidth (Miller effect on the CE stage) for each. Coupling and bypass capacitors and their effect on low-frequency response. Multistage and the Darlington/cascode configurations. The differential pair (foundation of op-amps and comparators). Power BJTs and thermal considerations (SOA, thermal runaway).
- **Why it matters / connections:** The BJT is the canonical active device and the differential pair is literally the input stage of the op-amp (Module 9). Common-base and cascode are RF/low-noise building blocks (→ 08). Biasing discipline (Q-point thinking) transfers directly to MOSFETs (Module 7). Emitter followers are the buffer you reach for to drive a load without disturbing a source.
- **Hands-on / exercises:** Hand-design a voltage-divider-biased CE amplifier for a target gain and Q-point; compute Z_in, Z_out, and midband gain. **LTspice:** build it, run `.op` to check the Q-point, `.ac` for gain/bandwidth, and `.tran` to see clipping at large signal; compare CE vs. emitter-follower output impedance driving a load. **Bench:** build the CE amp, measure gain and the −3 dB bandwidth, observe the Miller-limited high-frequency rolloff.
- **You've got it when:** You can bias a BJT stage for a stable Q-point without looking it up, predict its gain and impedances, and explain why the emitter follower has gain ≈ 1 but is still useful.

### Module 7: MOSFETs and FET Circuits

- **Concepts:** MOSFET structure (enhancement vs. depletion, N- and P-channel); threshold voltage V_th, the triode/linear and saturation regions; square-law I_D = ½ k(V_GS − V_th)² and the modern reality of short-channel/velocity-saturation deviations. Transconductance g_m, output resistance, body effect. **Biasing** FET amplifiers (self-bias, voltage-divider). Small-signal model. **Amplifier topologies**: common-source (≈ CE), common-drain/source-follower (≈ emitter-follower), common-gate (≈ common-base); gains and impedances. The very high gate input impedance and what it buys you (sensor/electrometer front-ends). JFETs (low-noise, high-Z amplifiers). **The MOSFET as a switch**: R_DS(on), gate charge, switching losses — the foundation of digital CMOS (→ 03) and switch-mode power (Module 13). Power MOSFETs, gate drive, and the body diode. CMOS inverter as the analog-to-digital bridge. Comparison table: BJT vs. MOSFET (input Z, noise, drive, speed, current handling).
- **Why it matters / connections:** MOSFETs run the modern world: every digital gate (→ 03), every switching regulator (Module 13), and most analog ICs. High-Z FET inputs are the right choice for high-impedance sensors (→ 04 embedded sensor interfacing). The CMOS inverter is the literal seam between analog and digital. Gate-drive and switching-loss reasoning is essential for modern power design.
- **Hands-on / exercises:** Hand-bias a common-source amp; derive its gain. **LTspice:** simulate a CS amplifier and a source follower; simulate a MOSFET low-side switch driving an inductive load and observe the body-diode/flyback behavior (add a freewheeling diode and compare). **Bench:** build a CS amplifier; use a MOSFET to switch an LED/motor from a microcontroller-level gate signal and measure R_DS(on) effects.
- **You've got it when:** You can choose BJT vs. MOSFET for a given job with reasons, bias a CS stage, and explain gate charge / switching loss in a power-switch context.

### Module 8: Operational Amplifiers — Ideal and Real

- **Concepts:** The op-amp as a near-ideal high-gain differential amplifier. **Ideal assumptions** (infinite gain, infinite Z_in, zero Z_out, infinite bandwidth) and the two **golden rules** with negative feedback (V+ = V−, no input current). Core configurations derived from scratch: inverting, non-inverting, voltage follower/buffer, summing, difference, integrator, differentiator, current-to-voltage (transimpedance) and voltage-to-current converters. **Non-idealities that bite in practice**: finite open-loop gain, input offset voltage and bias/offset currents, input/output voltage swing limits, **finite gain-bandwidth product (GBW)** and the gain–bandwidth tradeoff, **slew rate** and large-signal limits, CMRR, PSRR, input noise (ties to Module 14), and stability/phase margin (ties to Module 12). **Comparators** (open-loop, the importance of hysteresis/Schmitt trigger, why a regular op-amp is a poor comparator). The **instrumentation amplifier** (three-op-amp topology, high CMRR, gain set by one resistor) for small differential signals on a common-mode background. Single-supply vs. dual-supply design and virtual ground. Modern part selection (precision, low-noise, zero-drift/chopper, rail-to-rail, auto-zero op-amps).
- **Why it matters / connections:** The op-amp is the universal analog building block and the most likely thing a modern analog/mixed-signal role expects you to wield fluently. Transimpedance amps read photodiodes (→ 08 optical/detector front-ends); instrumentation amps read bridges and sensors (→ 04 embedded); the integrator is a filter and a control element (Module 12). Non-ideal effects (offset, GBW, slew) are exactly where rusty intuition fails — drill them.
- **Hands-on / exercises:** Derive gain for inverting/non-inverting/difference/summing by applying the golden rules. **LTspice:** build each config; then deliberately expose non-idealities — measure bandwidth shrinking with gain (GBW), trigger slew-rate limiting with a fast large step, and add offset to see DC error; build a comparator with and without hysteresis and observe chatter on a noisy input; build a 3-op-amp instrumentation amp and measure CMRR. **Bench:** build a non-inverting amp and measure its bandwidth at two gains to confirm GBW; build a Schmitt trigger and observe the hysteresis window.
- **You've got it when:** You can derive any standard op-amp config instantly, predict the closed-loop bandwidth from GBW and gain, and explain three reasons a real op-amp departs from ideal in a given design.

### Module 9: Passive and Active Filters

- **Concepts:** Filter fundamentals: low-pass, high-pass, band-pass, band-stop/notch, all-pass; passband, stopband, cutoff/−3 dB, rolloff order and slope, ripple. **Passive filters** (RC, RL, LC) and their loading problems. **Active filters** with op-amps: Sallen–Key and multiple-feedback topologies. Standard **approximations** and their tradeoffs: **Butterworth** (maximally flat), **Chebyshev** (steeper, passband ripple), **Bessel** (linear phase / flat group delay), elliptic/Cauer (steepest, ripple in both bands). Filter order and cascading; Q of a stage. Group delay and phase response (why it matters for pulses and data). Notch filters and the twin-T. **Switched-capacitor** filters (clock-tunable). Brief on the **digital filtering** alternative and the analog anti-aliasing filter that *must* precede any ADC.
- **Why it matters / connections:** Filtering is half of analog signal conditioning. This module is the explicit **analog→DSP bridge**: the same H(jω)/pole-zero thinking carries into digital filter design (→ 03/09), and the **anti-aliasing filter** is the mandatory analog guard in front of every sampled system (→ 04 embedded ADC front-ends, → 08 radar IF/baseband). Bessel vs. Butterworth (phase vs. magnitude) is a real design decision in pulsed/radar systems.
- **Hands-on / exercises:** Design a 2nd-order Butterworth low-pass Sallen–Key for a given cutoff by hand. **LTspice:** build Butterworth, Chebyshev, and Bessel low-pass of the same order and overlay their magnitude *and* group-delay responses; design a multiple-feedback band-pass and measure its center frequency and Q; cascade two stages to get a 4th-order response. **Bench:** build the Sallen–Key, sweep it, and confirm the cutoff and rolloff slope.
- **You've got it when:** You can pick the right filter family for a spec, design a Sallen–Key to a cutoff frequency without a tool, and explain why you'd never feed an ADC without an anti-aliasing filter.

### Module 10: Oscillators and Waveform Generation

- **Concepts:** The **Barkhausen criterion** (loop gain = 1, phase = 0°/360°) and the idea of intentional positive feedback at one frequency. Amplitude stabilization and why a real oscillator needs a nonlinearity/AGC. **RC oscillators**: Wien bridge, phase-shift. **LC oscillators**: Colpitts, Hartley, Clapp (tank-based, RF). **Crystal oscillators** (quartz Q, frequency stability, the most important precision-frequency source). Relaxation oscillators (op-amp/555-based square/triangle generation). **Voltage-controlled oscillators (VCO)** and varactor tuning. **Phase-locked loops (PLL)** conceptually (phase detector, loop filter, VCO; frequency synthesis) — flag as a modern/RF essential. Jitter and phase noise (ties to Module 14 and to → 08 radar, where oscillator phase noise sets coherent performance limits).
- **Why it matters / connections:** Every clock, carrier, and local oscillator starts here. Crystal/PLL frequency synthesis is the heartbeat of embedded systems (→ 04) and the local oscillator of every radar/comms front-end (→ 07/08). Oscillator **phase noise** is a first-order limit on radar Doppler and coherent processing — a direct connection to your background. The same loop-gain math is the flip side of feedback stability (Module 12).
- **Hands-on / exercises:** Analyze a Wien-bridge oscillator's frequency and gain condition by hand. **LTspice:** simulate a Wien bridge (with an amplitude-limiting element) and a Colpitts; build a 555 astable and a relaxation oscillator and measure frequency vs. RC. **Bench:** build a Wien-bridge or phase-shift oscillator, observe startup and amplitude settling, measure frequency, and look at the spectrum on a spectrum analyzer.
- **You've got it when:** You can state and apply the Barkhausen criterion, design an RC oscillator to a target frequency, and explain why crystal oscillators dominate where frequency accuracy matters.

### Module 11: Feedback and Stability

- **Concepts:** Negative vs. positive feedback; the general feedback equation A_cl = A/(1 + Aβ) and the benefits of negative feedback (gain stability, bandwidth extension, distortion/noise reduction within the loop, impedance modification). The four feedback topologies (series/shunt at input and output) and how they shape impedances. **Loop gain** (Aβ) as the central quantity. **Stability**: the s-plane, poles, and the condition for stability; **Bode stability criterion**, **gain margin** and **phase margin**; **Nyquist** view conceptually. Compensation: dominant-pole compensation, why op-amps are internally compensated, and the consequences (GBW tradeoff). Stability of unity-gain buffers driving capacitive loads (a classic real-world trap) and isolation-resistor fixes. Settling time and the relationship of phase margin to overshoot/ringing.
- **Why it matters / connections:** Feedback is the organizing principle behind nearly every precision analog circuit and is the deep link between analog electronics and **control systems** (and thus toward 09/10 and any modern signal-chain/power-control role). Phase-margin reasoning explains real failures: oscillating "amplifiers," ringing regulators, and unstable buffers. The same loop-gain math powers oscillators (Module 10) — stability and oscillation are two sides of one coin.
- **Hands-on / exercises:** Derive how negative feedback widens bandwidth (constant GBW) and lowers output impedance for a CE/op-amp stage. **LTspice:** measure loop gain and phase margin of an op-amp circuit (break-the-loop / Tian probe method); make a unity-gain buffer ring by adding a capacitive load, then stabilize it with a series resistor; observe overshoot vs. phase margin. **Bench:** drive an op-amp follower into a cap load and watch it ring, then tame it.
- **You've got it when:** You can read phase margin off a loop-gain Bode plot, predict overshoot from it, and diagnose/fix a circuit that oscillates when it shouldn't.

### Module 12: Power Supplies and Voltage Regulation

- **Concepts:** Unregulated supply chain refresher (transformer → rectifier → filter, Module 5). **Linear regulators**: series-pass concept, the classic three-terminal regulators, **LDOs** (dropout voltage, why low dropout matters), load and line regulation, PSRR, thermal design and dropout/efficiency limits. **Voltage references** (bandgap, buried Zener; accuracy, drift, noise). **Switch-mode power supplies (SMPS)** — flag as the dominant modern approach: buck, boost, buck-boost topologies; duty cycle, inductor/cap selection, continuous vs. discontinuous conduction, switching frequency, efficiency, and the **EMI/ripple** cost; synchronous rectification. Isolated topologies (flyback) conceptually. Linear vs. switching tradeoffs (efficiency, noise, complexity) and when low-noise applications still demand a linear post-regulator. Power sequencing, soft-start, inrush, and protection (OVP/OCP/thermal). Battery and supply rail decoupling (bulk + local ceramics, ties to Module 2 ESR/ESL and Module 15 layout).
- **Why it matters / connections:** Every system needs clean power, and SMPS literacy is now table stakes — but so is knowing when a noisy switcher will wreck a sensitive analog/RF front-end (→ 08) and must be followed by an LDO. Decoupling discipline directly affects digital signal integrity (→ 03) and embedded reliability (→ 04). Reference accuracy bounds every ADC/DAC's absolute accuracy.
- **Hands-on / exercises:** Hand-calc an LDO's power dissipation and heatsink need for a given drop and current. **LTspice / vendor tools (e.g., TI WEBENCH, ngspice):** simulate a buck converter, observe inductor current ripple and output ripple, and sweep load; compare a linear vs. switching solution for the same rail. **Bench:** measure line and load regulation of an LDO; look at switcher output ripple and switching spikes on the scope.
- **You've got it when:** You can choose linear vs. switching for a rail and justify it, size a buck inductor/cap to a ripple spec, and explain when to cascade a switcher into an LDO for a sensitive load.

### Module 13: Noise in Electronic Circuits

- **Concepts:** Fundamental noise sources: **thermal/Johnson–Nyquist** noise (v_n² = 4kTRB — memorize), **shot** noise (i_n² = 2qI_DC B), and **flicker/1/f** noise (low-frequency, device-dependent), plus burst and avalanche noise. Noise as a spectral density (V/√Hz, A/√Hz) and integrating over bandwidth. Adding uncorrelated noise sources in quadrature (RMS sum). **Signal-to-noise ratio (SNR)**. **Noise figure (NF)** and **noise factor (F)**; equivalent noise temperature. The **Friis formula** for cascaded stages and the dominant role of the first stage — *the* reason a low-noise amplifier (LNA) goes first. Amplifier input-referred noise (voltage and current noise) and noise matching / optimum source impedance. Noise bandwidth vs. −3 dB bandwidth. Practical noise reduction: bandwidth limiting, low-noise device/topology choice, cooling, correlated double sampling/chopping, and good grounding/shielding (Module 15).
- **Why it matters / connections:** This module is the explicit **low-noise-design → radar-front-end** bridge. Noise figure, Friis cascade, and "first stage dominates" are the core of receiver sensitivity (→ 08), and the same SNR thinking sets the detection floor in any sensing system (→ 04, → 09). It's also where rusty intuition is most valuable to rebuild for the current RF/instrumentation job market.
- **Hands-on / exercises:** Compute the thermal noise voltage of a resistor in a given bandwidth; compute the cascaded NF of a 3-stage chain with Friis and see how a poor first stage dominates. **LTspice:** run a `.noise` analysis on an op-amp amplifier, identify the dominant contributor, and observe how source resistance and bandwidth change total output noise. **Bench (if equipment allows):** view the noise floor of an amplifier on a spectrum analyzer; observe how reducing bandwidth lowers integrated noise.
- **You've got it when:** You can compute resistor thermal noise from memory, apply Friis to argue for an LNA-first chain, and explain why narrowing bandwidth improves SNR.

### Module 14: Real-World Parasitics, Grounding, and Layout

- **Concepts:** Why ideal schematics lie. Parasitics: trace resistance and inductance, parasitic capacitance, lead/package inductance, capacitor ESR/ESL and inductor self-resonance (revisit Module 2). **Grounding**: single-point vs. multipoint, **ground loops**, star grounds, separate analog/digital grounds and where to tie them, ground bounce. **Decoupling/bypassing** strategy (bulk + local, placement, loop area). **Return-current paths** and why the return path under a high-speed trace matters (the seed of transmission-line/signal-integrity thinking → 03/07). Crosstalk (capacitive and inductive) and mitigation. **EMI/EMC** basics: emission and susceptibility, shielding, ferrites, common-mode chokes, and the regulatory reality. PCB layout for analog/mixed-signal: component placement, Kelvin/sense connections, guarding high-impedance nodes, keeping switching loops tight, and partitioning. Thermal layout (copper pours, vias). Brief on transmission-line effects at high frequency (when a wire stops being a wire — explicit pointer to → 07).
- **Why it matters / connections:** This is the difference between a circuit that works in SPICE and one that works on the bench — the most common place experienced engineers separate from textbook ones, and a frequent interview/whiteboard topic. Return-path and parasitic reasoning is the direct on-ramp to signal integrity (→ 03) and transmission lines (→ 07), and good layout is what lets a low-noise front-end (Module 13) actually achieve its noise figure (→ 08).
- **Hands-on / exercises:** Annotate a schematic with the parasitics you'd expect to matter and where you'd place decoupling. **LTspice:** add trace inductance and cap ESL to a decoupling network and watch the high-frequency impedance and supply-noise rejection degrade; model a ground-loop EMF coupling into a sensitive node. **Bench:** demonstrate a ground loop with two grounded instruments and a long cable; show how a ferrite/common-mode choke cleans it up; probe with short vs. long ground leads to see the ringing difference.
- **You've got it when:** You can predict where a "working" schematic will misbehave on a board, place decoupling sensibly, and explain a ground loop and its fix.

### Module 15: Measurement and Instrumentation

- **Concepts:** **Digital multimeter (DMM)**: V/I/R/continuity/diode/capacitance, true-RMS vs. average-responding, input impedance and burden voltage, accuracy/resolution/counts, when the DMM loads the circuit. **Oscilloscope**: bandwidth and rise-time relation (BW ≈ 0.35/t_r), sample rate and aliasing, analog vs. digital storage, triggering (edge/pulse/etc.), coupling (AC/DC), **probes** (1×/10×, probe loading, compensation, ground-lead inductance), differential and current probes, FFT math. **Function/arbitrary waveform generator**: waveforms, output impedance (the 50 Ω convention and the 2× amplitude gotcha), modulation, sweep. **Spectrum analyzer**: frequency-domain measurement, resolution bandwidth, noise floor, dynamic range, measuring harmonics/spurs/phase-noise (ties to Modules 10, 13, → 08). **Bench power supply** (CV/CC, current limiting). LCR meter, network analyzer (VNA) overview (→ 07). Calibration, measurement uncertainty, and the discipline of trusting/verifying instruments — your radar-tech background is an asset here.
- **Why it matters / connections:** You cannot design or debug analog without trusting your instruments and knowing how they lie (loading, aliasing, probe effects). This skill set is directly transferable to and from your radar maintenance experience and is assumed in any analog/RF/embedded role (→ 04, → 07, → 08). The frequency-domain (spectrum analyzer/FFT) skills connect to DSP (→ 03/09).
- **Hands-on / exercises:** Measure the same signal with average-responding vs. true-RMS interpretation and explain the error on a non-sinusoid. **Bench:** compensate a 10× probe; deliberately under-sample a fast signal to see aliasing; measure a function generator's output into 50 Ω vs. high-Z and reconcile the 2× amplitude; capture a square wave's harmonics on a spectrum analyzer / scope-FFT. **LTspice:** generate signals and run the FFT to predict what you'll see on the analyzer.
- **You've got it when:** You can choose the right instrument and settings for a measurement, predict and avoid loading/aliasing/probe artifacts, and read a spectrum (harmonics, noise floor, spurs) confidently.

## Capstone / integrative exercise

**Design, simulate, and bench-build a low-noise sensor/receiver signal chain — the analog front end you'd put in front of an ADC.** This deliberately exercises nearly every module and mirrors a real analog/mixed-signal and radar-adjacent design task.

Target system: condition a small, high-impedance sensor signal (e.g., a photodiode via transimpedance, or a mV-level bridge via an instrumentation amp) riding on a common-mode background and noise, and deliver a clean, band-limited, properly-scaled signal ready for sampling.

Required stages and the modules they exercise:
1. **Front-end gain stage** — transimpedance or instrumentation amp, chosen and noise-optimized for the source (Modules 8, 13). Justify the device choice (low-noise op-amp / FET input) using input-referred noise and source impedance.
2. **Filtering** — an active anti-aliasing low-pass (and band-pass if appropriate) designed to a cutoff and order, with a justified filter family (Butterworth vs. Bessel for phase) (Module 9).
3. **Reference + power** — a clean rail: design a regulator (LDO, or a buck followed by an LDO for a noisy environment) and a reference, with full decoupling (Modules 12, 2, 14).
4. **Stability & noise budget** — verify phase margin of every feedback stage (Module 11), and compute the **cascaded noise figure / input-referred noise budget** with Friis, showing the first stage dominates (Module 13).
5. **Layout & grounding plan** — a written grounding/decoupling/return-path strategy and parasitic annotation (Module 14).
6. **Verification plan** — what you'd measure on DMM/scope/spectrum analyzer to prove each spec, including how you'd avoid loading/aliasing/probe artifacts (Module 15).

Deliverables: a hand-analysis noise/gain budget, a full **LTspice (or ngspice)** simulation of gain, bandwidth (`.ac`), transient settling (`.tran`), loop stability, and noise (`.noise`), and — if a bench is available — a built prototype with measured gain, bandwidth, noise floor, and regulation compared against prediction. Write a one-page design rationale connecting each choice to its module. **Modern flag:** explicitly note where you'd choose an integrated low-noise front-end IC or a switching-then-linear power architecture, as a current-market design would.

## Common pitfalls & rust-knockers

- **Forgetting the j / sign conventions:** dropping the sign on inverting gain, mixing up the j in Z_C = 1/(jωC) vs. Z_L = jωL, or losing track of the passive sign convention. Re-derive, don't recall.
- **Confusing the diode/transistor models:** using the constant-0.7 V model when small-signal r_d matters, or treating β as a precise constant when biasing (it isn't — that's why you use voltage-divider bias).
- **Treating the op-amp as ideal where it isn't:** ignoring finite GBW (expecting full gain at high frequency), slew-rate limiting on large fast signals, input offset/bias-current DC errors, and using a plain op-amp as a comparator (no hysteresis → chatter).
- **Ignoring loading:** forgetting that a real source/meter/next-stage loads the previous one; the classic high-impedance-node-measured-with-a-1MΩ-meter error.
- **Resonance/Q sign errors and units:** mixing f and ω, or BW = f₀/Q vs. f₀·Q.
- **Stability blind spots:** assuming negative feedback is always stable; driving a buffer into a capacitive load and getting ringing/oscillation; forgetting phase margin entirely.
- **Noise mistakes:** adding noise sources linearly instead of in quadrature; forgetting bandwidth in thermal-noise calc; not realizing the first stage dominates the cascade NF.
- **SPICE-only confidence:** trusting a simulation that has no parasitics, no ESR/ESL, ideal grounds, and ideal op-amps — then being surprised on the bench. Always sanity-check against real-world Module 14 effects.
- **Instrument self-deception:** average-responding meter on a non-sinusoid, aliasing on the scope, long ground leads causing ringing, the 50 Ω vs. high-Z function-generator 2× amplitude gotcha, uncompensated 10× probe.
- **Rusty mental arithmetic:** not being able to do quick dB conversions (×2 ≈ 6 dB, ×10 = 20 dB), 1/(2πRC) cutoffs, or load-line/Q-point estimates by inspection — drill these until they're automatic again.

## Self-assessment checklist

- [ ] I can analyze any linear DC network by nodal/mesh, superposition, and Thevenin/Norton by inspection or systematically.
- [ ] I can compute impedance of R/L/C vs. frequency instantly and reason about real-component parasitics (ESR/ESL, self-resonance).
- [ ] I can work AC problems in the phasor domain and sketch/read Bode plots fluently.
- [ ] I can derive RC/RL/RLC transient and resonant behavior (τ, ζ, ω₀, Q, bandwidth) both by calculus/Laplace and by intuition.
- [ ] I can design diode circuits: rectifier+filter to a ripple spec, Zener/clamp/limiter, and choose Schottky vs. PN.
- [ ] I can bias and small-signal-analyze BJT and MOSFET amplifiers and pick the right topology and device for a job.
- [ ] I can derive and apply every standard op-amp configuration and predict the effect of GBW, slew rate, offset, CMRR, and stability.
- [ ] I can design passive and active filters to a spec and justify the filter family; I never feed an ADC without anti-aliasing.
- [ ] I can apply the Barkhausen criterion and design RC/LC/crystal oscillators; I understand VCOs/PLLs and phase noise conceptually.
- [ ] I can read phase/gain margin from loop gain, predict overshoot, and fix an unstable circuit.
- [ ] I can choose and design linear vs. switching power, size a buck's L/C, and know when to cascade switcher→LDO.
- [ ] I can compute thermal/shot/flicker noise, apply Friis/noise figure, and design a low-noise (LNA-first) chain.
- [ ] I can predict where a working schematic fails on a real board (parasitics, grounding, return paths, EMI) and lay it out sensibly.
- [ ] I can choose and operate DMM/scope/function-gen/spectrum analyzer correctly and avoid their measurement traps.
- [ ] I completed the capstone front-end design with hand analysis, SPICE, and (if possible) bench verification.

## Canonical resources

**Core textbooks**
- *The Art of Electronics*, 3rd ed. — Horowitz & Hill. The single best practitioner-oriented reference; pair with *The Art of Electronics: The x-Chapters* for advanced/modern depth.
- *Microelectronic Circuits* — Sedra & Smith. The rigorous device/amplifier reference (BJT, MOSFET, op-amps, filters, feedback).
- *Fundamentals of Electric Circuits* — Sadiku & Alexander. Clean refresher for Modules 1–4 (DC/AC/transients/phasors).
- *Microelectronics* — Razavi (and Razavi's *Design of Analog CMOS Integrated Circuits* for modern IC-level depth).

**Noise, feedback, and design practice**
- *Low Noise Electronic System Design* — Motchenbacher & Connelly (noise, Module 13).
- *Noise Reduction Techniques in Electronic Systems* — Henry Ott (grounding/EMI/layout, Module 14).
- Analog Devices and TI application notes and handbooks: ADI *Op Amp Applications Handbook* (Jung), TI's op-amp/precision/power and "Analog Engineer's" pocket references — excellent, free, and modern.

**Power**
- *Fundamentals of Power Electronics* — Erickson & Maksimović (SMPS, Module 12).

**Simulation and tools**
- **LTspice** (Analog Devices) — free, industry-standard for these labs; **ngspice/KiCad** as an open alternative; **TI WEBENCH / TINA-TI** for power and quick part-level design; **Falstad Circuit Simulator** for fast intuition-building animations.

**Courses (video/online)**
- MIT OCW 6.002 *Circuits and Electronics* (fundamentals) and Berkeley/edX analog/microelectronics courses; All About Circuits (free text + practice); EEVblog (Dave Jones) for bench/instrumentation and real-world practice.

**Bench (modern, accessible)**
- A USB/benchtop oscilloscope, function generator, DMM, and bench supply; an **Analog Discovery 2/3** (scope + function gen + logic + network analyzer in one) is an excellent modern all-in-one for a home lab and directly supports nearly every lab above.
